How Might We Redesign the Griffith Observatory Website
to Provide Quick and Feasible Information?

Griffith Observatory's website is designed to provide visitors with information, such as directions, hours, and ongoing events. I collaborated and led with my team through research, ideation, and design through a desktop and mobile version of the Griffith Observatory.

Problem

Griffith Observatory's website is designed to provide visitors with information, such as directions, hours, and ongoing events. Users were unable to locate this information due to unclear navigation, lack of hierarchy, and a confusing display of content. Our team set out to redesign the Griffith Observatory so users are able to locate and access important information about the landmark in a quick and feasible manner.

Research

Research took place to identify user frustrations and insights on how to improve and reiterate the Griffith Observatory website. User interviews were conducted, hueristic evaluations were analyzed, and data was collected/cleaned.

Interview Notes

User interview questions were created to conduct 6 interviews to gather data about users demographics, what they knew about the Griffith Observatory, and what sort of information they’d like to obtain from the Griffith Observatory’s homepage.

Prioritization
Matrix

The team analyzed the data collected using a prioritization matrix. Data was categorized from High Complexity, High Impact/Priority, to Low Complexity, Low Impact/Priority.

Lo-Fi
Prototype

With Mobile First theory, our team decided to tackle the mobile version first. Using our User Flow as a guide, a Lo-Fi Mobile Prototype was designed with the intention of usability testing and how users would interact with the product.

Lo-Fi
Usability Test

User tests were conducted to research and verify the usability of our Lo-Fi mobile prototype. Following these tests, the team took in the data to reiterate the prototype to create a mid-fi prototype.
